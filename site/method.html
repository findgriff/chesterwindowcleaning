Method · Pure water-fed pole

How pure water cleaning works.

A long pole, a soft brush and water that dries to a streak-free finish.

Tap water has dissolved minerals in it — the white spots you see on a shower screen after a few weeks. Run that same water through a multi-stage filter (carbon → reverse osmosis → de-ionising resin) and you get water with nothing left in it but H₂O.

That water grabs onto dirt and dissolved minerals on your windows, and dries leaving nothing behind. No detergent. No squeegee. No streaks.

Why I work this way

  • No ladders. Everything reached from the ground, including upstairs windows and the awkward bits over conservatory roofs.
  • Eco. No chemicals run off onto your plants or down the drain.
  • Faster. Three minutes per window without repositioning a ladder.

Pure water vs the traditional squeegee

A squeegee and soapy water still works fine on the inside of glass — but outside, it has two problems. The soap residue it leaves behind actively attracts the next layer of dirt, and reaching upstairs windows means a ladder against your wall every single visit. Pure water does the opposite: because nothing is left on the glass, windows stay cleaner for longer between visits, and the frames and sills get washed as part of every clean rather than just the glass.

How often should windows be cleaned?

For most homes around Chester, every 4–6 weeks keeps glass looking consistently clean — that's the rhythm my round runs on. Near a main road, a building site or under trees, dirt builds faster and the regular cycle matters more. The first clean takes the longest because pure water pulls months of built-up mineral deposits out of the frame seals, which is why first and one-off cleans are priced separately. After two or three regular cleans the frames stop bleeding dirt and every visit dries perfectly.
